Housing Thesis

Why attainable build-to-rent is the defining opportunity in residential real estate

Lone Tree was founded on a simple observation: working families across America's secondary markets have nowhere to live. Homeownership is out of reach, subsidized housing has years-long waitlists, and institutional multifamily developers bypass smaller cities entirely. Build-to-rent fills that gap — and the data supports the thesis at scale.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about attainable housing and our approach

What is attainable housing and why is it important?

Attainable housing targets working families earning $45K–$95K annually — teachers, healthcare workers, first responders, and manufacturing employees who earn too much for subsidized housing but are priced out of new-construction homeownership. These households represent the largest underserved segment of the American housing market, with a nationwide shortage of 4+ million units in this income band. Lone Tree builds purpose-designed rental communities that serve this demographic without relying on government subsidy.

What is build-to-rent (BTR) housing?

Build-to-rent is a purpose-built residential product designed from the ground up as rental housing — typically single-family detached or cottage-style homes in community settings with shared amenities. Unlike scattered-site single-family rentals or apartment complexes, BTR communities offer the space, privacy, and lifestyle of homeownership with the flexibility and maintenance-free benefits of renting. The product type has seen significant institutional adoption over the past five years due to favorable demand fundamentals and attractive risk-adjusted returns.

How does Lone Tree partner with cities to build housing?

Lone Tree engages municipalities 12–18 months before land acquisition through structured meetings with planning departments, economic development authorities, and city councils. This collaborative process identifies local housing gaps, target demographics, preferred site locations, and design guidelines. The result is developments that cities actively welcome — accelerating entitlements, reducing political risk, and producing housing that genuinely serves community needs rather than imposing a one-size-fits-all product.

What is the investment structure for Lone Tree projects?

Lone Tree structures investments as LP/GP equity partnerships under Regulation D 506(b) private placements. Typical deal structures include a 4–5 year hold period, 8%+ preferred return to limited partners, an 80/20 profit split above the preferred return, and 75–80% loan-to-cost senior debt leverage. Each project is individually underwritten and offered as a standalone fund. Full offering documents, projections, and due diligence materials are available through our investor portal.

Why does Lone Tree focus on secondary and tertiary markets?

Secondary and tertiary markets — such as Richmond and Elizabethtown in Kentucky — offer compelling risk-adjusted returns due to lower land basis, less institutional competition, strong employment anchors (universities, regional healthcare systems, manufacturing), and growing populations. These markets often have significant housing supply gaps that larger developers overlook, creating opportunity for disciplined operators with local relationships and a willingness to engage at the municipal level.

What makes Lone Tree different from other BTR developers?

Three things distinguish Lone Tree: first, our city partnership model ensures every project is designed in collaboration with local government, producing housing that communities genuinely need and welcome. Second, vertical integration — we control land acquisition, entitlement, construction management, and property management in-house, eliminating the margin leakage and misalignment that comes from outsourcing to third parties. Third, our focus on secondary markets allows us to acquire land at a fraction of primary-market cost while serving demographics with stable, recession-resilient demand.
